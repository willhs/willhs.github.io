How could we compress a film to less than 1 MB while preserving the most quality?

This seemed like an interesting thing to try to learn about what video and audio models are capable of.

Normally when want a smaller video we do things like lower the frame resolution (1080p, 480p, etc), and audio sample rate (160 kbps is Spotify's High quality), and maybe the frame rate (movies and tv are usually 24fps). But can we crank those down enough for sub-1 MB? We'll see why it's not enough in the next section.

In this article I'll explore how we can compress films & TV using models to summarise films (+ normal compression) to sub-1 MB, and then re-generate the film from its compressed form. I'll be optimising for the best quality for the lowest $ cost.

### Shot by shot

Since video models generate short videos of around 5-10 seconds and shots in films tend to be a similar length, it would seem that video models could be used to generate a film as a sequence of shot videos. 

It turns out there's a Python library for finding shot boundaries in films! [PySceneDetect](https://www.scenedetect.com/).

Star Wars, for example, has around 2,000 shots which are detected pretty well by this library. There are a few exceptions, like the full-screen laser flashes in the first scene causing a few erroneous extra shots to be detected.

<aside class="aside note">

Note: it turns out the short output length of video models isn't as much a constraint as I thought because of the `first_image` param, more on that later.

</aside>

`ffmpeg` is a popular toolset for working with videos. You can use it to extract all sorts of things from videos like individual frames, audio, and clips.

### Describe each shot

Instead of compressing a film by reducing the pixels or sampling rates, we'll use a vision model: Google's *Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite* to summarise each shot in the film, given a few frames sampled from the shot. This model is cheap at $0.10 per million input tokens and $0.40 per million output tokens — scanning a full 2-hour film costs around $0.67.

### What about audio?

Movies have a few layers of audio: dialogue, music, and sound effects.

We'll at least split up dialogue from music and sound effects. 

I'll cheat a bit with dialogue by directly using the subtitles for the film, which are sometimes embedded in the film itself.

Now we're ready to generate a video for each of these shots with a video generation model, before stitching them together to create the movie, whole again!

### Selecting a video model

I'm going for the lowest cost for best quality movie in 480p+.

The best model quality VBM as of writing is ByteDance's *Seedance 2*, but it's too expensive at around $2,200 for 2 hours of output. It's also not open-weight so we'd have less control of model variants like quantised models.

Alibaba's Wan 2.1 & 2.2 are older models from early/mid 2025. The quality is noticeably worse but they're much cheaper to run, and open-weight. The smaller 1.3B model can run slowly on Macbook and the 5B model can fit on rented hardware like an Nvidia RTX 4090 GPU.

### The continuity problem: every shot, a different actor

<figure>
  <img src="/lossy/continuity-grid.jpg" alt="Four reconstructed frames of C-3PO from the same Lars homestead scene in Star Wars IV. He is a different gold droid in each — a different head and face every time." />
  <figcaption>C-3PO across four shots of one scene (Star Wars IV, shots 279–285), all generated by the same Wan 2.1 model. He's a different gold droid in every shot — a new head, a new face — because the model only ever saw a fresh ~100-word description of the shot, never the droid it had already drawn.</figcaption>
</figure>

Since we're stitching together clips that are generated independently, continuity becomes a major challenge in the way of making something coherent.

Something that surprised me about video models was how small the input prompt is. The sweet spot for models generally is on the order of *50 - 150 words*: 

<figure class="prompt-example">
  <div class="pe-card">

That's the budget we have for describing a shot in our compressed movie. So if we want Luke Skywalker to look the same across the clips we're generating we may only have 100 words to describe Mark Hamill's face, expression, pose, costume, etc. Other characters might be in the shot too.

### Music

For shots where <code>music</code> is dominant (judged by YAMNet), Gemini listens to the original audio and writes a one-line description of the score. Only the vibes are preserved, not the score.

On decode that sentence is fed to Meta's text-to-music 2023 model <a href="https://github.com/facebookresearch/audiocraft">MusicGen</a>, which invents a fresh score to match. 

> "A slow, ethereal, and slightly ominous orchestral score begins to swell. It features sustained strings and a subtle, rising synth pad, creating a sense of anticipation and foreboding. The tempo is very slow, around ~60bpm, and the music builds tension."

### Reflections

What astonishing film compression! We took a 632 MB film down to ≈320 KB, that's about 2,000× smaller. 

Now we can carry 3,000 films on a 1 GB USB drive, and to watch one of them we'll only need to best-case pay ≈$18 and wait 12 hours for processing.

Our decompressed films are *lossy*. But the output is indeed a full-length 480p (or up to 1080p for more $) video which retains core aspects of the film including all dialogue, and a basic visual and audio reconstruction (incl. some of the music & sound effects) of every shot in the input film.

For real though: the continuity issues we talked about earlier make the output film chaotic and disjointed. Every shot is from a different universe where the movie was made with a different crew, actors, etc. Most of the artistic detail of the film is lost in the compression process and made up by language models.

To improve it I'd probably focus on improving the consistency/continuity between clips generated by the LM.

Perhaps we'd get better output from our compressed form of the film by using it to re-shoot the film with a different crew, actors, etc. It would certainly handle the awful continuity problem we struggled with. But we're on a budget and can't afford to spend any more or wait any longer to watch our 1mb films.
