How could we compress a film to \<1mb while preserving the most quality?

We know about bitmap resolution (1080p, 480p, etc), frame rate (movies and tv are usually 24fps), and audio sample rate (160kbps is Spotify's High quality), but can we crank those down enuogh for sub-1mb? We'll explore that dead-end next.

Shakespeare's plays have been re-enacted for a few hundred years with nothing but screenplays which each probably fit into 1mb, but they're plays, not films or tv shows which are much more detailed, like instantiations of plays.

Modern-day multi-modal models make my mind melt. Apparently what started as *language* models sprouted the ability to do vision and audio too?? So I'm using this 1mb movie framing as an excuse to learn about MMMs, and to try writing.

I'm hoping you'll learn something and MMMs and/or be entertained 🙏

### Shot by shot

Since VLMs (video language models) generate short videos of around 5-10 seconds and shots in films tend to be a similar length, it would seem that VLMs could be used to generate a film from a sequence of shots. 

It turns out there's a python library for finding shot boundaries in films! [PySceneDetect](https://www.scenedetect.com/).

<aside class="aside note">

Note: it turns out the short output length of VLMs isn't as much a constraint as I thought because of the `first_image` VLM param, more on that later.

</aside>

`ffmpeg` is a popular toolset for working with videos. You can use it to all sorts of things like extracing audio from video, also extracting images and clips.

### What about audio?

Movies have a few layers of audio: dialogue, music, and sound effects.

We'll at least split up dialogue from music and sound effects. 

I'll cheat a bit with dialogue by directly using the subtitles for the film, which are sometimes embedded in the film itself.

#### Listening for sounds

We can use an audio classification model like [YAMNet](https://tfhub.dev/google/yamnet/1) to detect which of 521 everyday sound classes are present at which times
