How could we compress a film to \<1mb while preserving the most quality?

Dimensions that we normally compress videos on are bitmap resolution (1080p, 480p, etc), frame rate (movies and tv are usually 24fps), and audio sample rate (160kbps is Spotify's High quality), but can we crank those down enough for sub-1mb? We'll look at that dead-end in the next section.

Shakespeare's screenplays each probably fit into 1mb, but they're plays, not films or tv shows which are more detailed, like a single re-enactment of a play.

Multi-model language models are ideal for the compression aspect of this task as they're known to be good at summarising information, and can work with image and audio inputs. MMMs are also ideal for the de-compression too because they're a relatively fast and low-cost way to generate video and audio.

I'll use this challenge as a way to explore multi-modal models, something which seems a bit magical to me: apparently what started as *language* models sprouted the ability to do vision and audio too??

In this article I'll go through my experience and learnings to achieve radical compression through language models for the lowest cost.

### Shot by shot

Since VLMs (video language models) generate short videos of around 5-10 seconds and shots in films tend to be a similar length, it would seem that VLMs could be used to generate a film as a sequence of shot videos. 

It turns out there's a python library for finding shot boundaries in films! [PySceneDetect](https://www.scenedetect.com/).

Star wars, for example has 2000 clips (to make precise) which are detected pretty well by this library. There are a few exceptions, like the full-screen laser flashes in the first scene causing a few eroneus extra shots to be detected.

### Describe each shot

Instead of compressing a film by reducing the pixels or sampling rates, we'll use an image LM: Google's *Gemini Flash 2.5 Lite* to summarise each shot in the film, given a few frames sampled from the shot. This gemini flash lite model is cheap at $0.10 per million input tokens and $0.40 per million output tokens.

### Selecting a video model

I'm going for the lowest cost for a plausable movie output in 480p/720p.

The best model for output quality is currently ByteDance's Seedance 2 model, but it's too expensive at around $1200 for a 2 hour movie length output. It's also not open-weight so we'd have to choose from a few hosting providers.

Alibaba's Wan 2.1 & 2.2 are older models from early/mid 2025. The quality is noticeably worse but it's dramatically cheaper to run. It's open-weight and its ~10GB (to confirm) RAM fits on an Nvidia GTX 4090 GPU which is meaningful for self-hosting.

### Self hosting

Using model cloud services like fal.ai and replicate.ai are easier to use because they handle the hosting for you. You pay for output: most hosted video models bill **per second of generated video** (roughly $0.05–0.50/s depending on the model and resolution), so a clip costs that rate times its length — and since many models only emit fixed 5–10s clips, it ends up feeling like a flat price per clip. Replicate also offers a second model for open-source checkpoints: **per second of GPU time** (cost = render time × hardware rate), which is less predictable because you're paying for however long the model takes to run, not for the video you get back.

Self-hosting can be cheaper. In our case we want the best cost for generating a large number of clips, so the per-hour pricing of self-hosting can be better than the per-clip pricing of the model service APIs.

### The continuity problem: every shot, a different actor

<figure>
  <img src="/lossy/continuity-grid.jpg" alt="Four reconstructed frames of C-3PO from the same Lars homestead scene in Star Wars IV. He is a different gold droid in each — a different head and face every time." />
  <figcaption>C-3PO across four shots of one scene (Star Wars IV, shots 279–285), all generated by the same Wan 2.1 model. Something that suprised me about VLMs was how small the input prompt is. The sweet spot for models generally is on the order of *50 - 150 words*. That's the budget we have for describing a shot in our de-compressed movie.

We want characters and objects that appear in more than one shot to look the same across the clips we're generating, but for example for a shot with Luke Skywalker from Star Wars we may only have 100 words to describe Mark Hamill's face, stature, pose, costume, expression, etc. This is a big problem.

### Reflections

What astonishing film compression! We took a 632 MB film down to under 1 MB, a compression ratio of roughly 1000×. 

Now we can carry 1000 films on a 1gb usb drive, and to watch one of them we'll only need to pay ~$35NZD and wait ~12 hours for processing. 

Caution: our decompressed films are and may be evident to be *lossy*. But the output is indeed a full-length high-def (up to 1080p) video which retains core aspects of the film including all dialog, and a basic visual and audio reconstruction (incl. some of the music & sound effects) of every shot in the input film.

Perhaps we'd get better output from our compressed form of the film by using it to re-shoot the film with a different crew, actors, etc. We surely would. It would certainly handle the continuity aspect we struggled with. But we're on a budget and can't afford to spend any more or wait any longer to watch our 1mb films.

I hope this was educational as a tour of some of what's possible with video models, and what's necessary (not much) to use them, especially on a budget.

Did we merely build a machine that turns art into slop? Judging by the output, maybe. But
